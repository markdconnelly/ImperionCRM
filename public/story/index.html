01
The spark

It started with a game

A friend was using Claude Code to build a prize game for RuneScape. The thought landed instantly: if it can do that, I can build a business application that delivers real value.

That night the target was a CRM to fix our sales pipeline — a single screen to stop losing deals in the cracks between tools. But the moment the first real data landed in a real database, the scope expanded on its own: if one agent could unify the pipeline, it could unify everything an MSP knows about a customer.

The ambition didn't stay small for long. Within days it was four repositories, one database, and a live product on Azure.

02
The problem

An MSP's knowledge is scattered and decaying

Running a managed-services business means knowing your customers cold. In practice that knowledge lives in ten disconnected places, and no one can see all of it at once.

Ten silos per contact — email, Teams, a text thread, a call recording, a LinkedIn comment, an Autotask ticket, someone's memory.
Reps walk in cold — hours of manual research and data entry an agent could have done overnight.
Profiling is legal risk — every text, call, and profile is a liability unless you can prove consent.
Tool sprawl — M365 + Kaseya + spreadsheets, re-keyed by hand, with no single source of truth.
03
What it is

One place to work. One agent to ask.

It started as a CRM and didn't stay one. Imperion OS is the AI-enabled platform an MSP runs its whole business on — CRM + ERP + a full AI suite on one surface — sitting as an intelligence layer above Microsoft 365 and Kaseya, augmenting them, never replacing them. It manages the whole lifecycle of a managed-services customer, from the first ad they see to the quarterly business review years later: lead → qualification → proposal → onboarding → implementation → operational readiness → continuous success — and the internal operations behind it: projects, delivery, time & expense, finance, and the service desk.

The promise is two-sided. One place to work — every account, contact, ticket, and conversation on one surface instead of ten. And one agent to ask — a single orchestrator that routes to specialist sub-agents behind the scenes, so the user never has to know which system an answer came from.

The defining go-to-market motion is assessment-led: a paid AI Security Readiness Assessment — six scored dimensions and a remediation roadmap — is the wedge that turns a cold prospect into a multi-year managed-services contract.

10
The method scales

One session became ten

The two-and-a-half-hour feature was one session driving one worktree. By that evening, that was the ceiling — not the model. The bottleneck had never been typing speed; it was how many independent changes one architect could keep coherent at once. So before bed, the method itself got an upgrade.

A short planning chat designed a concurrency contract: every session gets its own git worktree and its own dev port — s01→3001 … s10→3010, ten slots — shared hot files stay out of each other's lane, and the only counters GitHub can't hand out atomically, migration and ADR numbers, are claimed at merge instead of at authoring, so two parallel branches can never collide on the same number (ADR-0084). Ten sessions, one database, no stepped-on toes.

With the rails laid, ten coordinated sessions ran across all four repos at once. They didn't just polish time tracking — they carried it deeper (CFO payroll approval, the six-deviation reconciliation, a Time Efficiency BI section, inline admin correction) and stood up a whole second feature in the same night: employee expense tracking, end to end — ADR-0083, migrations 0088–0091, capture-to-reimbursement, with the matching backend writer, the bronze→silver merge, and the on-prem bulk pulls all filed alongside it.

Then the architect closed the laptop and went to bed — and the method had one more thing left to prove. Idea to a shipped feature in an evening, then one feature to a parallel two-feature build overnight, was the curve so far. The next question was sharper: could the whole thing be left entirely alone?

The bottleneck stopped being how fast people type and became how fast one person can decide. Parallelism only pays off if the parallel work can't corrupt itself — so the contract that prevents collisions shipped before the ten sessions did.

11
Planning is what makes AFK safe

He watched UFC 250, slept, and worked his day job. The robots didn't.

The parallel leap proved the method could fan out. The real test was whether it could run unattended — not one supervised evening at the keyboard, but a full day of the architect simply living his life. So that became the experiment. The fight card was on, then it was bedtime, then it was a full day at the actual MSP job. For most of a day, no one was watching the build.

It didn't slow down. In that ~24-hour window, governed sessions across all four repos opened, reviewed, and squash-merged roughly sixty pull requests — and none of it was filler. A near-complete project-management suite arrived in one stretch: sprints and backlog carry-over, baselines and planned-vs-actual, gantt / calendar / timeline views, task dependencies and subtasks, multiple assignees and watchers, @mentions, admin-definable custom fields, OKRs with rollup, workload and capacity planning, recurring tasks, form-to-task intake, kanban rich cards, and an in-app notification bell. Beside it, five more verticals broke ground — lead scoring, customer journeys, opportunity forecasting, e-signature, and conversational intelligence — plus DNS and security-posture drains, and the employee expense data-loop closed from capture to reimbursement.

None of it needed a human in the loop, because the loop is the repo. Every unit of work was an issue → a worktree → a branch → a reviewed micro-PR, judged against rules the agents re-read at the start of every session. And you can watch the plan defend itself in the git history: when two parallel branches reached for the same migration number, the concurrency contract caught the collision at merge and renumbered automatically — custom-fields 0102→0103, a 0104↔0105 cascade untangled, the freed gap back-filled — with no one awake to referee it. That is the entire thesis: the planning was paid up front, so the building could happen while the lights were off.

Unsupervised isn't ungoverned. The agents could be left completely alone precisely because the constraints weren't in anyone's head — they were committed to the repo: rule files, ADRs, a collision-proof numbering contract, and CI that fails closed. Plan once, then go watch the fight. Flipping anything to production data is still the only thing that waits for a human.

12
Four repos, one mind

The hard part isn't writing code. It's keeping four repos honest.

Speed in one repo is easy. The trap in a four-repo system is drift: the database moves in the front end, and three consumers — the backend's processes, the cloud pipeline's merge, the on-prem collectors — silently fall out of step. That's where most distributed builds quietly rot.

It didn't happen here, because the contract lives in the repo, not in anyone's head. Every repo carries a rule file the agent reads first; schema is proposed in one place (the front end owns migrations) and the siblings are declared consumers; one ADR number and one shared vocabulary thread through all four. The agent doesn't have to remember the architecture — it re-reads it every session and is held to it by CI.

The same time-tracking sprint shows it in motion: migrations 0085–0087 landed in the front end, and in the same push the matching epics and issues were filed across all three siblings — the backend writing idempotent Autotask Time Tickets on approval, the pipeline and on-prem collectors corroborating attendance. Four repos, never disagreeing about what a time_record means.

One agent held the whole system at once. What a team coordinates with meetings, the agent coordinates by reading the contract — so a schema change and its three downstream consequences ship as one coherent move, not a week of catch-up.

13
The method turns inward

Then it audited its own foundation

Shipping fast leaves a tax: the same shape gets written four slightly-different times in four repos, and a year later that's where the bugs live. So the method was pointed at itself. An automated architecture pass read all four repositories and surfaced nine places where a near-identical pattern had been copied at a seam — candidates to collapse into one deep, well-tested module with the variants behind config.

The interesting part wasn't the consolidating — it was the restraint. Each candidate was treated as a hypothesis, not a fact: a survey agent catalogued the real duplication before a line was changed. Seven held up and were built — the idempotency invariant that guards every state machine pulled into one shared transitioner, the pinned vector contract given a single machine-readable home with CI that fails the other repos on drift, the secret resolvers, the connector registry, the snapshot-diff. Two were refused: on inspection the "duplication" was three debuggable units that a merge would have turned into one bloated monolith, so they were closed with the reasons written down. A dead-code bug the audit turned up got fixed in the same pass.

And the lesson was made reusable, not just remembered: the deliberation ladder that decides when consolidating is worth it — and when leaving things alone is the better engineering — was committed as a first-class agent skill (ADR-0101), so the next session inherits the judgment instead of relearning it.

What disciplined autonomy looks like Nine consolidation candidates, surfaced automatically across four repos. Seven built, two deliberately refused — every public name kept as a thin adapter so not a single call site had to change, and every "no" recorded with its reasoning. The point of the pass wasn't to refactor everything; it was to know the difference between concentration that pays and abstraction that costs.

The hardest call an autonomous builder makes is not building. A model that only ever says yes will over-engineer a codebase as fast as it ships features. Knowing which two of nine to walk away from — and writing down why — is the same governance that lets the rest run unattended.

The reframe

We went looking for the state of the art in agent memory — and found we were 70% of the way there

Late in the build a question turned outward: how do the serious people give AI agents durable memory and context? So the method read the field — MemPalace, Open Brain (OB1), Claude-Mem, the Claude-Code-memory practitioners, and a run of talks on folder-as-architecture and agent loops. The blueprint they all describe was unmistakable: memory that is tiered, provenance-tagged, semantically grounded, citation-backed, and identity-scoped.

The surprise was how much of it already existed here — not as a memory product, but as plain data engineering. The medallion pipeline was provenance and freshness. The OKF semantic layer was meaning and authority — which source wins, how entities join. Gold + Voyage voyage-3-large @ 1024-dim vectors were citation-backed recall. A second brain for agents had been built without anyone calling it one. Roughly seventy percent of the blueprint was already shipped.

The last thirty percent was then built deliberately, not stumbled into: tiered knowledge — canon · company · personal, six personal brains; the three-axis RLS access spine (owner · company · data-class) that scopes every memory — and every action — to an identity; the 1–5 graduated-autonomy dial and approval cockpit (ADR-0107); the agent eval / quality plane; and the action / tool-grant and event / trigger governance planes.

Which forced the reframe. This was never a CRM with AI bolted on — the data substrate had been an operating system for agents the whole time. So the product took the name the architecture had already earned: Imperion OS (ADR-0110).

17
The second brain, built

The deliberate 30% — built, not just designed

The reframe said the platform was ~70% of the agent-memory blueprint already, with a deliberate 30% to finish. That 30% has now landed in the schema — migrations 0166–0170 applied to production, governed by four decision records (ADR-0113 → ADR-0116).

These four pieces now have one name: the Imperion OS Brain — the single memory-and-knowledge contract every agent reads and writes through. It is a hard requirement for v1, and it is treated as a contract, not a finished feature: the schema is provisioned, but the index is not yet hydrated, so what follows describes the shape the agents will think with, not a live recall yet.

What makes that contract safe is four invariants that hold no matter how the brain grows: a personal note can never cross into the company tier except through one privileged, append-only-ledgered curator; the shared memory tool can only write a caller's own personal scope; the three row-level-security axes fail closed; and personal, verbatim text stays out of the shared meaning layer — recall returns a citation and a summary, never raw words. The boundary is a database invariant, not a promise.

Verbatim memory · ADR-0113

Raw agent turns and deliberate captures land in bronze — agent transcripts in agent_message, human/agent notes in memory_drawer (0167) — never summarized. The reasoning substrate is always the gold summary; verbatim is what you drill to when the exact words matter.

Personal knowledge store · ADR-0114

Two substrates: a Postgres Synthesis Store — a per-owner temporal knowledge graph (0168) where a changed mind is a new fact with a validity window, never a destructive edit — and a per-owner Azure Blob Curated Vault of owner-edited markdown (0169), synced over HTTPS.

Gold hybrid ranker · ADR-0115

Recall scores semantic + full-text + metadata + temporal together (0166), not vector similarity alone — precise on exact identifiers, relevant on meaning, and recency-aware via the validity windows.

Universal Memory MCP · ADR-0116

One memory surface for every agent — store / recall / list_agents (0170) — over the same medallion kernel, scoped by the three-axis RLS spine (owner · company · data-class).

Honest about the substrate The memory substrate is real and provisioned — migrations through 0170 applied to production, the six per-owner vault containers, their RBAC, and the non-BYPASSRLS Personal Curator identity all live. It is not yet hydrated: the capture tables are empty and embedding generation (the gold vectors) has not been run, so semantic recall is the finished contract waiting on the on-prem collectors — deploy-dormant, not broken.

    FoundationThe web app, Entra SSO, PostgreSQL + pgvector, and the four-repo split that shaped everything after it.
  2. Jun 9–12
    Build-outEvery CRM module, the first integrations, and the reporting BI hub — carried across the line at speed.
  3. Jun 12
    Live integrationsMeta (Facebook/Instagram) flowing in production; Autotask ticket webhooks and M365 ingestion wired.
  4. Jun 13
    One evening → ten sessionsTime tracking built end-to-end in a single evening; that night a concurrency contract turned one session into ten.
  5. Jun 13–14
    The unattended day~60 reviewed pull requests overnight — a near-complete project-management suite, the expense loop, and five new verticals — with no one at the keyboard.
  6. Jun 14–15
    CRM parity waveConversational intelligence, opportunity forecasting, e-signature, marketing journeys, and a service desk shipped as schema + surface.
  7. Jun 16–17
    From CRM to a whole platformThe queued frontier shipped — premium service management (asset/identity CMDB + ITIL change enablement), an AR collections agent, a self-serve report builder, a connector marketplace, marketing journeys, and ICM glass-box autonomy. A CRM had become a full CRM + ERP + AI platform, with an onboarding-grade docs hub across all four repos.
  8. Jun 18
    The build audits itselfAn automated architecture pass swept all four repos for copied patterns — nine candidates, seven consolidated and two deliberately refused — while the expense and CMDB verticals deepened (out-of-pocket entry, Azure cloud-asset merge).
  9. Jun 19–21
    The reframe → Imperion OSA research pass on agent memory (MemPalace, OB1, Claude-Mem, the practitioners) found the platform was already ~70% of the blueprint. The deliberate 30% landed — tiered knowledge, the RLS access spine, the autonomy dial + cockpit, the eval and tool-grant/trigger planes — and the product was renamed Imperion OS (ADR-0110) across all four repos.
  10. Jun 22
    The second brain, builtThe deliberate 30% landed as schema: client mapping + entity resolution live, the AI-Technician wedge (Felix) and the eight-agent roster shipped, and the unified agent-memory substrate — verbatim bronze, a two-substrate personal store with a temporal knowledge graph, the gold hybrid ranker, and a universal memory MCP — applied to production (migrations 0166–0170, ADR-0113→0116), provisioned and awaiting hydration.
  11. Jun 23
    The foundation under the agentsThe data-trust and governance planes landed and applied to production: entity resolution and bitemporal cross-system keys, a data-quality SLA that gates autonomy, a governed metrics layer with per-client profitability & ROI, and a three-axis row-level-security spine (owner · company · data-class) with an audited admin god-view.
  12. Jun 23–24
    Connections rework & on-prem go-liveConnections and credentials were reworked into one card per connector with canonical Key-Vault naming and a remove-credential path that purges the secret — and the on-prem pipeline started running for real on the home server, ~75 scheduled collectors hydrating bronze and silver in production.
  13. Jun 24–25
    The v1 doctrine & the OS BrainThe path to v1 was settled as a written doctrine — agent-first plumbing, human-first operation, autonomy earned in graduated steps — and the Imperion OS Brain was named as the single memory/knowledge contract the whole platform converges on. Grounding-conflict resolution with audited write-back shipped alongside it.
  14. Now
    The wave trainFour concurrent lanes — front end · backend · cloud pipeline · on-prem — shipping a checked-off wave at a time, now also deepening the foundation under itself. The collectors are live and hydrating; the self-hosted Managed-Agents runtime is up; the remaining gates are activation settings and vectorization, not missing code.
